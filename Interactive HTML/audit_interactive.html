Summary: Digital Asset Audit Made Simple

Bank vs Crypto: The Vault Analogy
  • Bank: Cash is kept in a physical vault. Auditors count the cash and check the locks.
  • Crypto: Digital assets are kept in wallets (like digital vaults). Auditors check the blockchain and test who controls the keys.
Proof of Reserves: The Balance Analogy
  • Does the company have enough crypto to cover what it owes customers?
  • Auditors check the blockchain and company records to make sure the numbers match.
  • If the balance is short, it’s a big red flag.
How Auditors Check Crypto
  • Get a list of wallets (like account numbers).
  • Ask the company to prove they control the wallets (like signing a message or moving a small amount).
  • Look up wallet balances on the blockchain (public ledger).
  • Compare blockchain balances to company records.
AML: Stopping Bad Actors
  • Just like banks, crypto companies must check who their customers are (KYC).
  • They watch for suspicious transactions and report them.
  • Crypto adds extra tech to trace anonymous wallets and transactions.

Key Takeaways (Simple)

  • Crypto audits are like bank audits, but use blockchain instead of cash.
  • Auditors check who controls the wallets and if the company has enough assets.
  • Extra tech is needed to trace crypto transactions and stop bad actors.
  • If you understand bank audits, you’re halfway to understanding crypto audits!

Overview

Virtual Asset Service Providers operate in a regulated environment that combines traditional financial services with blockchain technology. As auditors, we apply established assurance principles while adapting our evidence-gathering and testing procedures to account for the unique characteristics of digital assets.

Regulatory Context

VARA (Virtual Assets Regulatory Authority) in Dubai has established a comprehensive regulatory framework that requires VASPs to maintain robust financial reporting, customer asset protection, AML/CFT compliance, and cybersecurity controls. Our audit engagements address both IFRS financial statement audits and regulatory attestation requirements.

What Needs to Be Done

A comprehensive VASP audit addresses seven core areas, each with specific objectives and testing requirements:

1. Financial Statement Verification

Objectives

Verify that digital assets are accurately recorded, properly valued, and adequately disclosed in accordance with IFRS.

Key Requirements

  • Confirm existence and ownership of all custodial assets
  • Validate fair value measurements at reporting date
  • Test completeness of customer liabilities
  • Verify segregation of customer vs proprietary assets
  • Assess adequacy of disclosures for digital asset holdings
Deloitte Approach: We independently query public blockchains to verify asset existence, obtain cryptographic proof of control, and reconcile on-chain balances to the general ledger and sub-ledgers.
2. Proof of Reserves Attestation

Objectives

Provide independent assurance that the VASP maintains sufficient digital assets to cover all customer balances (target: reserve ratio of 100% or greater).

Key Requirements

  • Obtain complete listing of all custodial wallet addresses
  • Request and verify cryptographic signatures proving control
  • Query blockchain at the specified cutoff date and time
  • Calculate total customer liabilities from the platform database
  • Compute reserve ratio and assess adequacy
3. AML/CFT Compliance Testing

Objectives

Assess the design and operating effectiveness of AML/CFT controls, including KYC, transaction monitoring, sanctions screening, and Travel Rule compliance.

Key Requirements

  • Test KYC onboarding procedures and documentation
  • Evaluate transaction monitoring system parameters and alerts
  • Review suspicious activity report (SAR) filings
  • Validate sanctions screening against OFAC and other lists
  • Assess Travel Rule implementation for cross-VASP transfers
  • Review blockchain analytics tool integration and usage
Risk Area: VASPs face heightened AML risk due to pseudonymous nature of blockchain transactions and potential for cross-border illicit activity. Robust controls are mandatory under VARA regulations.
4. Cybersecurity & IT Controls

Objectives

Evaluate IT general controls and cybersecurity measures protecting digital asset custody and trading infrastructure.

Key Requirements

  • Test access controls for wallet management systems
  • Review multi-signature approval workflows for cold storage
  • Assess hot-to-cold wallet sweep automation
  • Evaluate key generation and secure storage practices
  • Review disaster recovery and business continuity plans
  • Test incident response procedures
  • Assess penetration testing frequency and remediation
5. Governance & Oversight

Objectives

Assess board and management oversight, risk management framework, and compliance with VARA governance requirements.

Key Requirements

  • Review board composition and meeting minutes
  • Evaluate risk committee effectiveness
  • Assess internal audit function scope and independence
  • Review compliance function organization and reporting
  • Test three lines of defense model implementation
6. Customer Asset Protection

Objectives

Verify segregation of customer assets, test reconciliation controls, and assess insurance coverage.

Key Requirements

  • Confirm legal segregation of customer vs proprietary wallets
  • Test daily reconciliation of sub-ledger to blockchain
  • Review terms of service and custody agreements
  • Assess adequacy of insurance coverage (crime, D&O, cyber)
  • Evaluate customer complaint handling procedures
7. Market Conduct & Fair Dealing

Objectives

Test controls over trading operations, pricing, and prevention of market manipulation.

Key Requirements

  • Review trading surveillance system configuration
  • Test pricing methodology and reference data sources
  • Assess conflicts of interest policies (proprietary trading vs customer orders)
  • Evaluate disclosure of fees and charges
  • Review marketing materials for accuracy and compliance

Blockchain Verification Methodology

Step-by-Step Execution

Step 1: Wallet Address Collection

Obtain a complete listing of all wallet addresses under the VASP's control. This includes hot wallets, cold wallets, and any third-party custodian addresses.

Deloitte Procedure: Request a machine-readable export (CSV or JSON) containing all addresses, asset type, and purpose (customer custody, proprietary, operational). Verify completeness by reviewing wallet generation logs and comparing to prior period.

Step 2: Cryptographic Signature Request

For each wallet address, request that the VASP generate a signed message proving control of the private key.

Deloitte Procedure: Provide a unique nonce (e.g., "Deloitte Audit 2025-12-31 PoR Verification [random string]"). The VASP signs this message using each private key. We verify the signature using publicly available cryptographic libraries.

Step 3: Independent Blockchain Query

At the specified cutoff date and time, independently query the blockchain to capture balances for all verified addresses.

Deloitte Procedure: Use Deloitte-controlled nodes or trusted third-party API providers (e.g., Etherscan, Blockchain.com). Capture screenshots and export raw data. Archive blockchain block headers to establish cutoff timestamp.

Step 4: Reconciliation to Books and Records

Reconcile on-chain balances to the VASP's general ledger and customer sub-ledgers.

Deloitte Procedure: Obtain GL trial balance and customer account database export at cutoff. Sum customer liabilities by asset type. Compare blockchain-verified assets to GL. Investigate and resolve discrepancies (common items: in-transit transactions, incorrect block height, locked/staked assets).

Step 5: Reserve Ratio Calculation

Calculate the reserve ratio and assess adequacy.

Formula: Reserve Ratio = (Total Assets Verified on Blockchain) ÷ (Total Customer Liabilities per Sub-Ledger)
Target: ≥ 100%
Action if < 100%: Immediate escalation to engagement partner; consider qualification or disclaimer of opinion.

Step 6: Blockchain Reliability Assessment

Evaluate the effectiveness of the underlying blockchain technology and mechanisms that validate transactions.

Risk Factors to Evaluate:
  • Does the blockchain sufficiently check transaction integrity to prevent double-spending?
  • Does the incentivization structure encourage ethical behavior or enable malicious activity?
  • Is the blockchain sufficiently decentralized and proven in production use?
  • What is the consensus mechanism and historical uptime/security record?

Documentation Required: Blockchain reliability assessment memo retained in engagement files.

Audit Assertions for Digital Assets

Testing digital assets requires addressing the same financial statement assertions as traditional assets, but with blockchain-specific evidence and procedures.

Existence, Completeness, and Ownership (ECO)

Existence Testing

Objective

Verify that digital assets recorded in the financial statements actually exist and are held at the reporting date.

Four-Step Approach

  1. Understand and identify the population
    • Nature of digital asset types held or transacted by the entity
    • Management's accounting conclusions
    • Completeness and accuracy of population
  2. Determine method of storage
    • Self-custody or third-party custodian
    • Availability of public address information
    • Commingled or segregated wallets
  3. Define the test objective
    • Ending balance at period end (e.g., December 31, 20XX)
    • Transaction balance at point in time during audit period
    • Specific block height and timestamp
  4. Obtain sufficient audit evidence
    • Evaluate reliability of evidence obtained from blockchains
    • Obtain confirmations with third-parties; review SOC reports
    • Perform rollforward of activity using COINIA
Deloitte COINIA: Our proprietary public blockchain reader tool enables independent verification of digital asset balances and transactions without reliance on client-provided data.

Additional Considerations

When Substantive Procedures Alone May Not Provide Sufficient Evidence:
  • High transaction volumes make sampling impractical
  • Complex private key management requires control testing
  • Commingled wallet environments necessitate allocation control testing
  • Multiple custody arrangements increase control reliance needs

Response: Test operating effectiveness of controls over wallet creation, transaction authorization, and reconciliation in addition to substantive procedures.

Completeness Testing

Completeness of Balances and Keys

Completeness of Balances

Verify that all digital assets owned by the entity are recorded in the financial statements.

Testing Procedures

  • Test operating effectiveness of controls:
    • Digital asset reconciliation or rollforward controls
    • Controls over authorization of digital asset transactions
    • Controls over the creation of digital wallets
    • Controls over commingled wallets and segregation between corporate and customer balances
  • Independent rollforward recreation:
    • Obtain details of all digital asset transactions from the blockchain using COINIA
    • Reconcile the activity to the recorded balance
    • Investigate discrepancies (timing differences, locked assets, in-transit transactions)
  • Reciprocal population testing:
    • Build case of facts: wallets, transaction activity, contract terms
    • Identify reciprocal populations (independently pulled transaction data vs. client records)
    • Use open-ended confirmations requesting wallet addresses used
    • Test for presence of side agreements or undisclosed arrangements

Completeness of Keys (Self-Custody)

Obtain evidence that the list of private keys is complete.

  • Review wallet generation logs and compare to prior periods
  • Test controls over wallet creation and recordkeeping
  • Evaluate segregation between corporate and customer wallets
  • Assess commingled wallet allocation controls
  • Confirm no wallets have been created outside documented procedures
Designing Completeness Tests: Start with the client's wallet listing, then work backwards to verify completeness using independent evidence from blockchain queries, open-ended confirmations, and control testing.

Rights and Obligations (R&O)

Proving Ownership and Control

Fundamental Principle

The party that controls the private key owns the digital assets.

Key Questions to Address

  • How does the entity safeguard the private keys required to access their digital assets?
  • Can the entity demonstrate control of the private keys?
  • Are there any restrictions on the entity's ability to use the assets?
  • Are customer assets properly segregated from proprietary assets?

Testing Approach: Third-Party Custodian

Commingled Wallets

  • Obtain SOC 1 report (if available)
  • Confirm allocation methodology
  • Test entity's balance allocation controls
  • Verify segregation from other customers

Segregated Wallets

  • Confirm wallet addresses are dedicated to entity
  • Verify security over private keys
  • Test movement of funds (if applicable)
  • Confirm no side agreements exist

Testing Approach: Self-Custody

Establishing Evidence of Ownership

On-Chain Methods (Movement of Assets):

  • Observe live transfer of assets in and out of a wallet
  • Verify transaction hash using COINIA
  • Confirm sender and receiver addresses
  • Verify operational use aligns with policy matrix

Off-Chain Methods (Message Signing):

  • Request client to send a signed message from each tested wallet
  • Provide unique nonce (e.g., "Deloitte Audit 2025-12-31 [random string]")
  • Verify the signature using COINIA without moving funds
  • Confirm cryptographic proof of private key control

Control Testing Considerations (Self-Custody)

Focus Areas:

  • Quality of evidence obtained (stronger for controls testing in high-volume environments)
  • IT focus: systems, configurations, access controls
  • Policy-type controls to address segregation of duties
  • Testing is point-in-time; consider frequency and monitoring

Attribute Sampling Approach

Use nonmonetary attribute sampling when testing control effectiveness:

  • Key creation process: Test controls over when and how keys are created
  • Key encryption/decryption: Verify encryption standards and access controls
  • Transaction signing: Test authorization workflows and multi-signature requirements
  • Point-in-time nature: Document control operation at specific dates/times
Tips & Troubleshooting: Facilitate detailed follow-ups between COINIA specialists and the client's engineering team to understand cryptographic implementations and resolve technical questions.

Valuation Approach

Fair Value Measurement

1. Understand Client's Pricing Source

  • Evaluate relevance of their defined principal market
  • Assess pricing hierarchy (Level 1, 2, or 3 inputs)
  • Understand cutoff convention (UTC timestamp, block height, market close)
  • Review pricing policy documentation and approval

2. Independently Obtain Pricing

Deloitte Approach: Use vendor data from CoinMetrics or other approved sources. Vendor due diligence is performed centrally by our valuation specialists.
  • Obtain pricing for all digital assets held at reporting date
  • Use consistent timestamp with client's defined cutoff
  • Document pricing source and reliability assessment

3. Test Reasonableness of Pricing

  • Compare client pricing to independently obtained pricing
  • Investigate variances exceeding threshold (e.g., >5%)
  • Compare pricing trends to comparative currencies
  • Evaluate consistency of principal market definition
  • Test cutoff timing and verify alignment with blockchain timestamp

Allocation Testing (FIFO/Impairment)

Cost Allocation and Impairment

Rollforward Approach

Obtain the client's digital asset rollforward showing:

  • Beginning balance: Cost basis by asset type
  • Inflows (Day 1): Acquisitions, purchases, mining, staking rewards
  • Outflows (Day 2): Sales, transfers out, disposals
  • Ending holdings: Remaining cost basis and fair value

Testing Procedures

1. Test Pricing Application

  • Verify date and quantity of inflows from blockchain or confirmations
  • Test pricing applied to acquisitions (spot price at transaction date)
  • Evaluate reasonableness of Day 1 pricing

2. Test FIFO Allocation

  • Verify client has documented and consistently applied FIFO methodology
  • Sample transactions and recalculate cost of goods sold
  • Test allocation of earliest costs to outflows
  • Verify mathematical accuracy of rollforward

3. Test Approach Selection

Substantive Analytical Procedures

  • Design expectation based on weighted average activity
  • Calculate monthly or quarterly average pricing
  • Compare expected cost allocation to client's calculation
  • Investigate differences exceeding threshold

Test of Details

  • Sample individual transactions
  • Recalculate FIFO allocation
  • Verify pricing applied
  • Trace to source documents and blockchain

4. Leverage Transaction-Level Testing

  • Use ECO testing procedures to verify transaction dates and quantities
  • Cross-reference pricing applied in valuation to transaction-level evidence
  • Assess consistency of pricing methodology across transaction types
Note: Many entities use vendor solutions (e.g., Lukka, CoinTracker) for FIFO allocation and tax lot management. Evaluate the reliability and controls over these systems as part of IT audit procedures.

Revenue Auditing for VASPs

Transaction Revenue (Trading Fees)

Common Characteristics
  • High volume: Quantitatively small individual transactions aggregated to material revenue
  • Highly automated: Fee calculation engines process transactions in real-time
  • No estimates or judgment: Fee rates are pre-determined in terms of service
  • Established fee structure: Based on customer agreement or public fee schedule
  • No individually significant customers: Revenue is diversified across user base
  • Immediate authorization: Customers authorize trade terms prior to execution
  • Correlation with trading volume: Revenue should be predictable based on activity levels

Risk Assessment

Typically Lower Risk: Due to automation, lack of judgment, and high volume of small transactions.

Focus Areas: Completeness and accuracy of fee capture; proper functioning of IT application controls.

Gross vs. Net Presentation

Principal vs. Agent Considerations

Key Questions

Question Gross (Principal) Net (Agent)
Is another party involved in providing the digital assets? Entity sources and delivers assets Third party provides assets
Is the company primarily responsible for providing the digital assets? Yes - entity is responsible to customer No - facilitates transaction only
Does the company bear inventory risk? Yes - holds assets before sale No - matches buyers and sellers
Does the company have pricing discretion? Yes - sets or negotiates prices No - market determines pricing
Does the company control the crypto assets before delivery to customer? Yes - obtains control then transfers No - never controls assets
Is the company using inventory or matching trades? Using inventory (proprietary trading) Matching trades (exchange model)
What is the promise: matchmaking service or digital assets? Promise is to deliver assets Promise is to provide platform/matching
Common Scenarios:
  • Custodial exchange (matching): Agent - recognize net trading fees
  • Broker-dealer (inventory): Principal - recognize gross revenue and cost of sales
  • Custody-only: Agent - recognize custody fees as net revenue

Audit Procedures for Revenue

Testing Approach

Substantive Analytical Procedures

  • Obtain trading volume data by period (daily, weekly, monthly)
  • Obtain fee schedule by customer tier or transaction type
  • Recalculate expected fee revenue based on volume and rates
  • Compare expected revenue to recorded revenue
  • Investigate variances exceeding threshold
  • Evaluate trends and correlation with market activity

Test of Details

  • Sample individual transactions from blockchain or trading system
  • Verify fee calculation based on applicable fee schedule
  • Test allocation of fees between entity and customer
  • Verify revenue recognition timing (at point of trade execution)
  • Trace sampled transactions to revenue ledger

IT Application Controls Testing

  • Test controls over fee calculation engine configuration
  • Verify fee schedule updates are properly authorized and tested
  • Test interface controls between trading system and general ledger
  • Assess completeness of fee capture (no transactions bypassed)
  • Test accuracy of customer tier assignment

Blockchain Analytics Integration

We verify that the VASP has integrated blockchain analytics tools (e.g., Chainalysis, Elliptic, TRM Labs) and is actively using them to:

  • Screen deposits and withdrawals against known illicit addresses
  • Assess transaction risk scores based on on-chain activity
  • Investigate alerts flagged by the analytics platform
  • Generate reports for regulatory filings and internal escalation

Testing Procedures

  • Review tool configuration and rule sets
  • Test alert generation by selecting sample transactions
  • Verify analyst investigation and resolution documentation
  • Assess false positive rates and tuning activities
  • Confirm escalation to compliance and SAR filing where appropriate

Cybersecurity & Wallet Security Testing

Hot Wallet Controls
  • Maximum balance thresholds defined and monitored
  • Automated sweeps to cold storage configured and operating
  • Multi-factor authentication for all access
  • IP whitelisting and geographic restrictions
  • Real-time balance monitoring and alerts
  • Rate limiting on withdrawal transactions
Cold Wallet Controls
  • Air-gapped key generation and storage
  • Multi-signature approval (minimum 2-of-3 or 3-of-5)
  • Physical security (secure vault, biometric access)
  • Segregation of duties (no single person holds quorum)
  • Hardware security modules (HSM) for key storage
  • Regular key backup and recovery testing

Critical Control: Private Key Management

Loss or compromise of private keys can result in immediate and irreversible loss of digital assets. We perform detailed testing of key lifecycle management:

  • Key generation (entropy source, randomness)
  • Key storage (encryption, HSM, offline)
  • Key usage (approval workflows, logging)
  • Key backup (redundancy, geographic distribution)
  • Key recovery (tested procedures, documented responsibilities)
  • Key destruction (secure deletion, audit trail)

Deliverables & Reporting

Audit Opinion (IFRS Financial Statements)

Independent auditor's report expressing an opinion on whether the financial statements present fairly, in all material respects, the financial position and results of operations in accordance with IFRS.

Key Audit Matters (KAMs) - Typical for VASPs

  • Existence and valuation of digital assets
  • Completeness of customer liabilities
  • Revenue recognition for trading and custody fees
  • Going concern assessment (particularly for newer VASPs)
Timing: Issued within 90 days of year-end (subject to VARA filing deadlines)
Proof of Reserves Report

Independent attestation report confirming that the VASP held sufficient digital assets to meet customer obligations as of the report date.

Report Components

  • Scope and methodology description
  • Summary of assets verified by blockchain and asset type
  • Total customer liabilities by asset type
  • Calculated reserve ratio for each asset and in aggregate
